Cuba. Medio siglo entre la revolución y la dictadura

January 1st marks the fiftieth anniversary of the triumph of the Cuban Revolution. That is, half a century, an expression that better captures the passage of time. In 1959, no one would have bet on the revolution lasting so long. Not even Fidel Castro, who had repeatedly stated that his objective was to restore democracy to the country and reinstate the 1940 Constitution.

Since 1952, Cuba had been suffering under the dictatorship of Fulgencio Batista following a bloodless coup that was justified as "revolutionary" and "for the good of the nation threatened by partisan corruption." Given the almost universal indifference of Cubans, his rise to power was also tolerated by the United States.

Batista, a mulatto sergeant who had risen to the rank of general and become a strongman in the 1930s, held an ideology close to the populism of Perón or Getúlio Vargas. Therefore, his first announcements were housing for the poorest citizens and a general increase in wages. In fact, in 1940 he had been president of Cuba thanks to the votes of the middle class, which overwhelmingly supported his party, the Democratic Socialist Coalition. His program was based on order and public spending, but without concealing his affinity for socialism, a tendency confirmed by his appointment of two prominent communists, Juan Marinello and Carlos Rafael Rodríguez, as ministers.

### sobresalir

En 1958, sin embargo, la dictadura estaba a punto de ser derrotada debido (y quizá esto sea el «determinismo retrospectivo» del que hablaba Max Weber) a la corrupción, el rechazo popular y la represión descontrolada de la policía. Sin embargo, un motivo sobresalía por encima del resto: el deseo de libertad de los cubanos, cansados de las injusticias de Batista. Si a eso unimos la retirada del apoyo estadounidense y la presión guerrillera tendremos el cuadro completo de la caída del régimen en la madrugada del 1 de enero de 1959.

In 1958, however, the dictatorship was on the verge of collapse due (and perhaps this is the "retrospective determinism" of which Max Weber spoke) to corruption, popular rejection, and unchecked police repression. Nevertheless, one reason stood out above the rest: the Cuban people's desire for freedom, weary of Batista's injustices. If we add to that the withdrawal of US support and the guerrilla pressure, we have the complete picture of the regime's fall in the early hours of January 1, 1959.

Batista, heading for Santo Domingo, left behind a republic in turmoil and at the mercy of the 26th of July Movement. At that moment, the rebel forces had already reached the center of the country, although they did not control the main provincial capitals, much less Havana. Nevertheless, the revolutionaries triumphed, and they did so with the full support of the Cuban people, convinced that Fidel Castro was the leader who would savior Cuba.
